The Question We're Asked Most

Granite or Quartz — Which Is Right for You?

It's the single most common question we're asked in our Harlow showroom. Both granite and quartz make exceptional kitchen worktops. Both are durable, beautiful, and built to last decades. But they're meaningfully different materials — and the right choice depends on how you cook, how you live, and what you value most in a kitchen surface.

This guide gives you an honest, balanced comparison. We stock both materials and have no interest in steering you towards one over the other — only in helping you make the right decision for your home.

Know Your Stone

Understanding Each Material

Natural Stone

What Is Granite?

Granite is an igneous rock formed deep within the earth from slowly cooling magma. It's composed primarily of quartz, feldspar, and mica — minerals that give each slab its distinctive speckled or veined appearance. Because it's formed by natural geological processes over millions of years, no two granite slabs are identical. It's one of the hardest natural materials on earth, second only to diamond on the Mohs hardness scale.

  • Unique characterEvery slab is one of a kind — the veining, colour, and crystal patterns cannot be replicated.
  • Outstanding heat resistanceYou can place hot pans directly on the surface without risk of damage or discolouration.
  • Exceptional hardnessHighly resistant to scratching under normal kitchen use.
  • Long-term valueA genuine lifetime material — granite worktops regularly outlast the kitchens they're fitted in.
  • Natural & sustainableNo synthetic resins or chemicals — quarried and cut from the earth.
Considerations
  • Requires sealing on installation and resealing every 1–2 years
  • Porous when unsealed — can absorb liquids if maintenance is neglected
  • Heavier than engineered alternatives — may require cabinet reinforcement
  • Colour matching for extensions or repairs can be difficult
Engineered Stone

What Is Quartz?

Quartz worktops are an engineered material made from approximately 90–95% crushed natural quartz crystals bound together with polymer resins and pigments. Unlike granite, the manufacturing process allows precise control over colour, pattern, and consistency. The result is a surface that combines the hardness and durability of natural quartz with the practical benefits of a non-porous, sealed finish — straight from the factory.

  • Non-porous surfaceResins fill every gap, making quartz completely non-porous. No sealing required — ever.
  • Exceptional stain resistanceImpervious to wine, coffee, oil, and most household substances.
  • Consistent appearanceUniform colour and pattern throughout — ideal for matched surfaces across a large kitchen.
  • HygienicThe non-porous surface prevents bacteria and mould from taking hold.
  • Wide colour rangeAvailable in shades not found in nature, including pure whites and bold colours.
Considerations
  • Resins can discolour under prolonged direct UV exposure — not ideal for outdoor use
  • Less heat resistant than granite — trivets are advisable for hot pans
  • Pattern lacks the organic uniqueness of natural stone
  • Cannot be refinished or repolished if scratched at depth
The Detail

Head-to-Head Comparison

Click each category to see how the two materials compare in depth.

Granite

Granite scores 6–7 on the Mohs hardness scale — harder than most metals. It's exceptionally resistant to scratching, chipping, and cracking under normal kitchen use. Its weakness, if any, is at unsupported edges, which can chip under a direct heavy impact. Our installations are fully supported to minimise this risk.

Quartz

Quartz composite typically scores 7 on the Mohs scale, making it marginally harder than granite in most comparisons. The resin binder also adds a degree of flexibility that makes it slightly more resistant to impact cracking than some granites. Both materials are, in practice, equally durable for everyday kitchen use.

Granite

Granite is porous and requires sealing. We apply a professional-grade sealant on every installation, which lasts 12–24 months under normal use. Resealing is straightforward — a DIY job using an off-the-shelf impregnating sealer — but it must be done. A well-sealed granite worktop is highly stain and bacteria resistant; a neglected one is not.

Quartz

Quartz requires no sealing — it's non-porous by manufacture. Day-to-day cleaning is straightforward: warm soapy water or a mild kitchen cleaner is all you need. This makes quartz the lower-maintenance option, and a better fit for households that want a beautiful worktop without any annual upkeep routine.

Granite

Granite can withstand temperatures up to around 270°C — well above anything a domestic hob or oven will produce. You can place a hot pan, roasting tin, or cafetière directly on the surface without risk of burning, cracking, or discolouration. This makes granite the natural choice for avid cooks who want maximum freedom in the kitchen.

Quartz

The polymer resins in quartz can discolour or warp under sustained extreme heat. Most manufacturers rate quartz worktops safe up to around 150°C — adequate for most kitchen tasks, but not reliable for resting very hot pans directly on the surface over time. We always recommend using trivets with quartz, particularly near the hob.

Granite

A properly sealed granite worktop resists staining from most common kitchen substances — red wine, coffee, oil, and citrus juice. The key word is "properly sealed". If the sealant has worn and a liquid is left to sit, absorption is possible. Prompt clean-up and regular resealing keeps granite highly stain resistant throughout its life.

Quartz

Quartz's non-porous surface gives it a structural advantage here. Because there are no microscopic pores for liquids to penetrate, quartz is inherently stain-resistant regardless of maintenance. For the same reason, bacteria and mould have nowhere to harbour — making quartz the preferred choice for those with young children or who prioritise hygiene.

Granite

No manufactured material can fully replicate the visual depth of natural granite. The crystals catch light differently throughout the day; the veining is organic and unrepeated. For those who value the authenticity of natural stone — its story, its uniqueness, its imperfection — granite is unmatched. Every slab you see in our showroom is genuinely one of a kind.

Quartz

Quartz offers something granite cannot: complete consistency. For large kitchens, open-plan spaces, or island-to-perimeter matching, quartz ensures every surface looks identical. Modern manufacturing has advanced enormously — premium quartz ranges now convincingly replicate marble, calacatta, and other natural stones with remarkable fidelity. The colour range is also vastly wider, extending to pure whites and deep blacks not found in nature.

Granite

Entry-level granite can be very competitively priced. Exotic or rare granites — deep blues, vivid greens, or particularly dramatic veining — carry a premium. Overall, granite pricing is driven by rarity of the stone and complexity of the job. A standard kitchen typically starts from around £1,000–£1,500 fully fitted for granite.

Quartz

Mid-range quartz is generally comparable to mid-range granite. Premium branded quartz (Silestone, Caesarstone, Dekton) can be significantly more expensive. Budget quartz can be cheaper than budget granite. Factor in the long-term maintenance cost too: quartz's zero sealing requirement means no ongoing spend, while granite requires a low-cost sealant every couple of years.

Which Is Right for You?

Making the Right Choice for Your Kitchen

The best worktop material depends on your lifestyle, cooking habits, and what you value most. Here are three common profiles.

The Serious Cook

You use the hob constantly, rest hot pans freely, and want a worktop that can keep up without fuss. Heat resistance matters more than sealing routine.

Choose Granite

The Busy Household

Kids at the table, coffee spills, packed lunches — you need a surface that's easy to clean, hygienic, and genuinely low maintenance with no annual upkeep.

Choose Quartz

The Design-Led Kitchen

Aesthetics are your priority. You want a statement surface that draws the eye — either the raw, unrepeatable beauty of natural stone or a perfectly matched, contemporary finish.

Our View

There's No Wrong Answer — Only the Right One for You

After fitting hundreds of kitchens across Essex and Hertfordshire, our honest view is this: granite and quartz are both exceptional materials. Neither is definitively better. The right choice is the one that fits your life.

If you cook with real heat, value natural uniqueness, and don't mind a simple annual maintenance ritual — granite is a magnificent choice that will only look better with age. If you want zero-maintenance, absolute stain resistance, and consistent colour across a large kitchen — quartz will serve you better.

The best way to decide is to see both materials in person. Our showroom holds over 200 slabs of granite, marble, and quartz. You can feel the surface finish, see how the colour reads in different light, and talk to our team about what would work best in your specific kitchen. There's no pressure and no obligation.
